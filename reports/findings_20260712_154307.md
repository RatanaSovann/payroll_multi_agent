## Executive Summary

A compliance review of the January–March 2026 payroll dataset has identified three material issues with a combined financial exposure of **$78,546** across 900 pay run records covering 150 employees. Findings span Superannuation Guarantee (SG) underpayment, PAYG withholding inconsistencies, and unpaid super for casual employees — all rated **HIGH** risk. The estimated ATO SG Charge (SGC) penalty exposure, if the matters are not voluntarily disclosed, is **$52,847**. The highest-priority action is to initiate a voluntary disclosure to the ATO within 21 days and halt further payroll processing until SG entitlements for all 33 affected employees have been independently recalculated and rectified. Separately, the organisation has significant readiness gaps for the Payday Super regime commencing 1 July 2026.

## Finding 1: Super Guarantee Underpayment — HIGH

### Issue

The payroll system has failed to remit the correct SG contribution for **33 employees** across **198 pay runs** during the review period. The total SG shortfall is **$37,899**, representing an average underpayment of **$191 per affected pay run**. This is the single largest exposure item in this review and triggers mandatory SG Charge obligations under the *Superannuation Guarantee (Administration) Act 1992* (SGAA).

### Regulatory Basis

Under the SGAA, employers are required to contribute a minimum of **11.5% of ordinary time earnings** (OTE) to a complying superannuation fund for each eligible employee per quarter. Failure to meet quarterly SG obligations by the prescribed due date results in an automatic liability for the **SG Charge (SGC)**, which is calculated on a broader wage base than OTE and is **non-deductible** for income tax purposes. The SGC comprises:

- The unpaid SG shortfall;
- **Nominal interest** of 10% per annum on each shortfall; and
- An **administration fee** of $20 per employee per quarter.

Late or incorrect SG payments cannot be retrospectively corrected by making a late contribution — a formal SGC statement must be lodged with the ATO.

## Finding 2: PAYG Withholding Inconsistency — HIGH

### Issue

**14 employees** across **47 pay runs** have been identified with PAYG withholding amounts deviating materially from the correct ATO bracket amount, with a total withholding exposure of **$20,297**. Of the 47 affected pay runs, **27 are under-withheld** (creating potential employee tax liabilities at year-end) and **20 are over-withheld** (representing cash flow harm to employees). The average deviation is **27.6%** from the correct withholding amount, and the maximum deviation recorded is **39.9%** — nearly 40% above the ATO's prescribed rate.

### Regulatory Basis

Under the *Taxation Administration Act 1953* (TAA), Schedule 1, Division 12, employers are required to withhold amounts from salary and wages in accordance with the ATO's **Tax Withheld Calculator** and the applicable **Statement of Tax Table** for the relevant pay frequency. Employers that fail to withhold the correct amount are exposed to:

- **Penalties under s. 16-25 TAA** for failure to withhold correctly;
- **Shortfall penalties** on under-withheld amounts at rates of up to 75% in cases of recklessness or intentional disregard; and
- Director penalty liability where the employer's PAYG withholding obligations are not met.

Under-withheld employees may also face unexpected tax liabilities and interest at the time of income tax assessment, potentially triggering employee relations issues.

Possible root causes include incorrect tax file number (TFN) declarations applied, wrong pay frequency settings in the payroll system, or failure to apply updated withholding tables following ATO schedule updates.

## Finding 3: Missing Super — Casual Employees — HIGH

### Issue

**16 casual employees** have received **zero or insufficient SG contributions** across **96 pay runs** during the review period, resulting in a total super shortfall of **$20,350**. This cohort is a subset of the broader SG underpayment finding (Finding 1) and is called out separately given the distinct regulatory history and the disproportionate risk profile of casual workers in superannuation compliance reviews.

### Regulatory Basis

The **$450 per month earnings threshold** for SG eligibility was abolished effective **1 July 2022** under the *Treasury Laws Amendment (Enhancing Superannuation Outcomes For Australians and Helping Australian Businesses Invest) Act 2021*. From that date, all casual employees — regardless of earnings — are entitled to SG contributions at the applicable rate (currently 11.5%) on their OTE. There are no state-based carve-outs. Any payroll system or process that continues to apply an earnings threshold is non-compliant.

## Recommendations

The following actions are listed in priority order. Items 1–3 require immediate attention before the next pay run.

1. **Initiate voluntary disclosure to the ATO within 21 days.** Prepare and lodge SGC statements for all affected quarters. Voluntary disclosure reduces the penalty rate from 200% to 25% of the SGC liability — a saving of approximately **$30,290** based on current estimates. Engage your tax adviser to prepare the disclosure documentation.

2. **Suspend payroll processing for the 49 affected employees** pending recalculation of all SG and PAYG entitlements. Do not attempt to correct shortfalls via the next pay run without first confirming the correct payable amounts through an independent recalculation.

3. **Audit the OTE definition configured in your payroll system.** The scale and consistency of SG underpayments across full-time employees (e.g., Callahan: $3,145; Montoya: $2,514) indicates the system is likely excluding allowances or other components that constitute OTE under the SGAA. Confirm the OTE definition with your payroll provider and cross-reference against the ATO's *Superannuation Guarantee Ruling SGR 2009/2*.

4. **Remove any residual $450/month earnings threshold logic from the payroll system** for casual employee super calculations. This threshold was legislatively abolished on 1 July 2022. Any active filter suppressing super on low-earning casual pay runs must be disabled immediately.

5. **Investigate and correct PAYG tax table configuration.** The consistent, department-wide deviation from correct withholding rates (average 27.6%) indicates a systemic tax table or pay frequency configuration error. Engage your payroll system vendor to audit the withholding calculation engine and verify alignment with the ATO's current Schedule 1 withholding tables.

6. **Remediate over-withheld PAYG amounts for affected employees.** The 20 over-withheld pay runs represent cash that has been incorrectly deducted from employee take-home pay. Review whether correction can be applied in-cycle or requires a supplementary payment, and communicate proactively with affected employees.

7. **Manually review the 24 records with null `payg_deviation_pct` values.** These records could not be assessed for PAYG compliance and may conceal additional withholding anomalies. Treat as unreviewed until cleared.

8. **Implement a fortnightly three-way payroll reconciliation control**: gross wages × 11.5% SG rate vs. super paid; ATO withheld calculator output vs. PAYG withheld; and prior period vs. current period variance analysis. These controls should be embedded as a mandatory pre-approval step in the payroll sign-off process.

9. **Conduct a comprehensive SG audit for the prior financial year (FY2025)** to determine whether the identified underpayment patterns predate the current review period. Given the systemic nature of the failures, prior year exposure is likely.

## Payday Super Readiness

The **Treasury Laws Amendment (Better Targeted Super and Other Measures) Act** introduces **Payday Super**, effective **1 July 2026** — fewer than four months from the end of this review period. Under Payday Super, employers will be required to pay SG contributions to employees' superannuation funds **within one business day of each payroll payment**, compared to the current quarterly remittance cycle.

This organisation's current compliance profile presents a **critical readiness risk**. Specifically:

- **Systemic SG calculation failures** affecting 33 employees and 198 pay runs demonstrate that the payroll engine cannot currently be relied upon to produce correct super entitlements on a real-time basis.
- **Casual super exclusions** that are already non-compliant under the post-July 2022 rules will be immediately visible to the ATO under Payday Super's real-time data reporting, significantly increasing detection risk.
- **No evidence of automated SG validation controls** exists in the current payroll process — a control environment that is insufficient for quarterly compliance is entirely inadequate for a daily obligation.

The following Payday Super readiness actions should be initiated **no later than 1 April 2026**:

- Complete payroll system reconfiguration and SG calculation audit by 30 April 2026;
- Select and onboard a compliant Payday Super clearing house or fund integration pathway by 31 May 2026;
- Conduct end-to-end parallel testing of real-time contribution flows by 30 June 2026; and
- Establish daily SG reconciliation reporting within the payroll function.

Failure to be operationally ready by 1 July 2026 will expose the organisation to penalties under the new Payday Super enforcement framework from day one.

## Data Quality Notes

The following caveats from the data validation phase should be noted when interpreting the findings above:

1. **Seven source columns were unmapped from the canonical analysis schema** — specifically `period_number`, `pay_period_start`, `pay_period_end`, `sg_compliant`, `sg_shortfall`, `payg_deviation_pct`, and `payg_flagged`. These appear to be pre-calculated compliance fields generated upstream. The compliance analysis in this report has been performed independently against core wage, super, and PAYG fields; however, the derivation logic for these upstream-calculated fields has not been independently verified and should not be relied upon without confirmation of the source calculation methodology.

2. **24 null values in `payg_deviation_pct`** (~2.7% of the dataset) mean that the PAYG withholding analysis in Finding 2 is necessarily incomplete. The 14 employees and 47 pay runs flagged represent a minimum — the true number of non-compliant PAYG records may be higher. These 24 records require manual review as a priority.

3. **Pay period boundary dates were not independently validated.** The alignment of `pay_period_start`, `pay_period_end`, and `pay_date` fields with ATO-prescribed pay cycle requirements has not been verified in this review. Any SG shortfall figures that are quarter-boundary sensitive (i.e., where contributions span two SG quarters) should be recalculated to confirm correct quarterly allocation before SGC statements are lodged.

4. **No Modern Award coverage assessment was possible** for the six departments in scope (Customer Service, Finance, HR, IT, Operations, Sales). Award mapping against supported awards could not be completed due to the use of department names rather than employment classifications or award codes. A separate Modern Award compliance review is recommended once position-level data is available.
